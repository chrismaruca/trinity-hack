Design

  1. Explain the main design goals your team had for the gauntlet:

    • Design a gauntlet that can provide important data on the surrounding environment:
      • Object detection for low visibility conditions
      • Temperature and humidity measurement to detect fires
      • Gas measurement to detect gas leaks
    • Design a cool housing for all of our sensors and components on the glove
    • Create a website that receives and displays data from sensors on the gauntlet
    • Have our website look nice and be easy to navigate
    • Have fun!

  2. Explain why you chose the sensors and how that would benefit your mission:

    Upon entrance into the competition, teams weren’t told directly what they’d be put up against. However, we were given clues; most notably, we were given a scenario where “bruin engineers” are trapped in a space which could be dark and subject to high temperatures, seismic activity (shaking), as well as gas leaks and potential enemies. With this information in mind, we reasoned that the sensors/electronics mentioned below would serve us in the following ways:

    • Gas sensor: Given the direct hint that there would be “gas leaks”, we knew this sensor would be a necessity when it came to determining environmental gas safety when going in to rescue our fellow bruin-rescuees. While some could argue that the nose acts as a good sensory tool for determining toxic gas, it must be acknowledged that not all toxic gas can be easily smelled–especially if our e-Hero has a cold!
    • Temperature & humidity sensor: Given the uncertain mission-prompt, we initially sought to prioritize the other sensors, and opted out of focusing on the temp/humidity sensor. To specify, we figured that in a scenario where there is fire or hot temperatures, our bodies (sensory info from skin) would give us indication of temperature, and thus this sensor was low priority at the beginning–especially because some of us were not familiar with I2C. However, after finding out more details about the actual testing method, we decided to include it. In retrospect, we could have reasoned that accurate information about temperature and humidity could have indeed helped us in terms of determining more details about the environmental gas safety, as moisture and temperature affect the behavior of fluids. With this information, a well-educated mission control team could do further analysis on the environmental gas safety.
    • Ultrasonic sensor: Alongside the Ring LED, we figured that including an ultrasonic sensor could help us both navigate “dark room” scenarios (to see if an object is obstructing us), as well as giving us long range data (≤13ft) about whether or not an enemy or fellow bruin rescuee is in front of us, given the scenario. Given more time and some stepper motors, we could have even used the glove as a stationary sentry device (one where the U.S. is attached to a stepper motor which scans the environment and feeds data back so that mission control and/or OLED can display a radar of our environment, like ones seen on boats).
    • Ring LED: Since we were unsure what “dark room” could mean in terms of testing, we decided to include the Ring LED in hopes that it would act as a flashlight to illuminate whatever “dark room” our e-Hero stepped foot in. However, once we got more details revealed, we realized it was not needed for that purpose, and included it in the glove for pure aesthetics
    • OLED screen (required electronic): Could give the e-Hero live feedback from the Ultrasonic sensor about distance or indicate if the environment was too toxic to be in. (At the start, we were unsure whether or not we could relay data from multiple sensors to the OLED, but after discovering we could, we opted to include data from all sensors in a readable format).

  3. Explain the code that you wrote and why you designed it in that way:

    • Website related: We used a combination of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript to format our website and make it function. The first order of importance was making sure that the website was able to receive the data from the ESP32, which we accomplished quickly thanks to the code provided by Austin. Another important piece of code in the website is the code that handles interpreting the data that is received from the ESP32. We did this using JavaScript’s MutationObserver, which checks for whenever a portion of the website is changed. One last important feature are the slideshows, which required portions of code in all three of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript to function. Once those three parts were out of the way, most of the other improvements and iterations on the site were aesthetic (backgrounds, positioning, et cetera).
    • Arduino related: The Arduino code is mainly functional, reading values from sensors and parsing them to suit our needs. The only code relating to aesthetics in the Arduino is for the OLED screen and ring LED. We knocked out the code that handled each sensor day by day, with us getting the ultrasonic sensor working on day 1, the temperature & humidity sensor on day 2, and the gas sensor on day 3. The most difficult thing to code in the Arduino was probably the aesthetic code for the OLED and LEDs because it required a lot of trial and error (with long compile times) and careful avoidance of any clashing with the functional code for the sensors.

  4. Explain problems that occurred and how you used the engineering mindset to help you debug the issue:

    • Website related: We encountered many problems relating to CSS and HTML on the website, like elements not appearing for seemingly no reason, elements being the wrong size, random spacing after images, and other unexpected behavior. When these problems happened, we were able to push through by staying calm, diagnosing the problem as precisely as we could, and searching for a solution. One particular problem was that the images on the website had a random space under them. To diagnose the problem we carefully looked at the elements involved in displaying the images and eventually singled it out to the element itself adding a space, which we found out was due to it having its display set to inline.
    • Arduino and sensor related: This being one of our first times working with the esp32 board it was a challenge to figure out the correct pins that would receive and output the correct data. Wiring everything was easier to figure out after multiple iterations, in the end we managed to color code all wires to the esp32 board with blue and yellow wires. Coding wise I referenced a lot of existing code online and had the help of my team to debug any problems I had. When one sensor failed, my previous knowledge from my intro to circuits class helped me to think there might be a shortage on the breadboard, which after staring at it for a good 10 minutes I managed to find the short and fix it to get all the sensors working together.

  5. Explain how you solve those problems in coding, circuits, and Arduinos, what were the iterations you made to improve on those designs?

    • Website related: We solved the problem using trial and error guided by our engineering knowledge. One particular problem was that the images on the website had a random space under them. To diagnose the problem we carefully looked at the elements involved in displaying the images and eventually singled it out to the <img> element itself adding a space, which we found out from a search was due to it having its display set to inline by default.

  6. Explain your iterative design CAD, include screenshots of before and after. Explain why you made those changes.

    Originally, our team wanted to prioritize functionality of the gauntlet over “cool factor”, especially because of the 48 hour constraint. We figured if we could reduce the risk in any department, and optimize time efficiency, we would be better off than the competition. Some aspects in pursuing this strategy were staying away from 3d printing, as each print carries with it both a risk of the print failing itself, as well as long print times (for designs which you sometimes cannot even verify if your idea works until after it has printed for a given duration) .Thus, we first opted for something which we could iterate upon, and chose a laser cut chassis with velcro straps on one side to secure it to the wrist; unfortunately, because there were only two velcro straps of minimal length, we had to come up with a design which secured the other non-velcroed side; it was this aspect which we had to iterate upon, especially for the “guide-rail” which can be seen in prototype one. The brackets and rail were both 3d-printed, which in itself grants room for sizing errors. As a solution, we 3d printed multiple cylinder sizes to test fit these brackets. However, as can be seen, “prototype 1” was not our final gauntlet. Ironically, we ended up going for the 3d printed route after observing our competitors consistently printing complex and aesthetic designs without 3d-print failure. We attempted this aspect on day 2, which was in itself a risky choice, and luckily this prototype required very little iteration due to proper dimensioning and forecasting prior to printing
